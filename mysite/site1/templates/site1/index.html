Biophysical Simulations

The study of physics at the nanoscale and in biology has been a topic of ongoing research in recent years due to the staggering array of structural variety that may be extrapolated to materials research and in the study of polymeric chemistry and pharmaceutical development. The inherent complexity of these nanoscale systems, stemming from the web of interactions involved in the manufacture and maintenance of structures such as micelles, vesicles, proteins and polymeric bilayers allow the wide range of phenomena seen in these soft structures. However, this very complexity also raises question into how to effectively analyse the root causes of the effects in question. For this reason, molecular simulation has been applied to model the nanoscale system of interest, as an additional angle of observation from traditional experimental methods, with unique advantages originating from the application of theoretical methods and machinery that would be physically impossible with traditional methods.

These systems in question do not reach the energetic levels of which the formation and breaking of covalent/ionic bonds become relevant - due to this nature, this field of research has been known to have soft interactions, and the field has been noted as soft matter. Molecular Simulations applied to Soft Matter Systems

As articulated before, the use of molecular simulation techniques has become increasingly prevalent both as a auxillary analysis method combined with traditional experimental methods, as well as a standalone methodology. In the past, the high convergence timescale of large, complex biophysical systems hindered it's use as a practical method to study scientific phenomena. Decades of technological advances, following the pattern of Moore's law, we have seen great improvements in computational power; by combining this with with techniques such as parallization of computer nodes to speed up the calculation of interactions/movements of systems, it has become possible to reach timescales where we can gain practical information that provide new insights. We alluded to the fact that the molecular interactions of SM do not involve energy scales for bond-forming/bond-breaking reactions, which would require complicated and computational demanding quantum mechanical calculations. Hence, by imitating classical dynamics, it is possible to accurately record the physical evolution of a system. Two practical methodologies are used for this purpose - the Monte-Carlo and Molecular Dynamics simulation methods. The former relies on the moving each molecular component based upon the most likely move based upon the boltzmann factor, while the latter method relies upon an iterative time-evoluton of Newton's equations of motion. The advantage and disadvantages of each method often depends on the specific system one analyses, and the development of either process is an ongoing field of research.



Clearly, the key problem of every complex simulation is this - How do we capture the accurate physics of a specific process of interest, within a reasonable timescale? Even with the computational resources avaliable at present, it can be difficult to reach a timescale for a reaction of interest to be observed, and then one must account for the limitation of only having a screenshot of a system. Complex processes such as protein conformational changes and protein-ligand interactions can only be observed through forcing the system to bias towards an environment where these changes can occur in the allocated time. Collectively, the change of state in these processes follow a pathway which are conveniently referred to as reaction coordinates, and shall be referred to as such. Many independent research groups have approached (add citations here) to develop collection of methods known as biased-sampling methods, which collectively have attempted to try to sample these high-energy states that constitute the reaction coordinate of interest. A number of options are avaliable for measuring the free energy change of a complex reaction coordinate. For example, metadynamics follows the approach of adding unfavourable gaussian potentials to previously mapped phase space, to ensure that the process maps new parts of the reaction coordinate, from which the free energy landscape can be recovered from the sum of the gaussian distributions. The most common method for calculating the free energy change, due to it's relative simplicity of implementation, and intuitive familiarity, is the umbrella sampling method. This involves dividing the reaction coordinate into discrete bins, and a biasing potential (usually in the form of hooks potential) is implemented to sample the rare-event trajectory incrementally. These new sampling bins are recombined and unbiased to produce the final free energy profile. Whilst this method has proven highly effective at producing the free energy profiles of many complex processes, such as ligand-docking simulations, protein conformational change and The primary non-equilibrium method mimicking atomic force microscopy (AFM)/force probe experiments has also been explored for free energy change - the steered molecular dynamics (SMD) method - by tethering a theoretical moving spring onto the molecule of question, this follows a continuous movement along the RC, which allows the calculation of the force, and hence, the work input. An interesting equality that relates work (a path integral function that is directionally dependent) with the free energy is the Jarzynski equality, which states that the mean of the exponential of work can be related to the exponential of the free energy. Due to the non-equilibrium nature of this equality, this suggests that the free energy can be calculated from the average of multiple fast-running simulations i.e. this is a parallel method of calculating the free energy, compared to the serial method of the umbrella sampling method.

In addition to enhanced sampling methods, an additional way to reach longer timescales has involved simplifying the molecular model itself. As of today, an extensive range of surfactants, lipids, and protein components have been parameterized in terms of molecular mechanics (MM), where the bond potential, angular potential, intermolecular and intramolecular potentials are given a highly detailed expression. Collectively, these are known as force fields (FF). The full atomic molecular description to include the full MM potentials in a FF comes with dramatic drawbacks - it limits the size of the simulation, which may produce an unphysical system, where the cut-off distances of each atomic component may be too large for the system, and as mentioned, the longer timescales required for convergence of properties may not be reached. To compensate for this, methods for simplification of the molecular model has been sought. For example, DPPC lipids and their derivatives (headgroup or tailgroup variation) have been characterized by Marrink et al using coarse-graining methods. CG methods involve simplifying the superfluous elements in an all-atomic (AA) simulation - for example, the interaction parameters in each CH_{x} component in the chain is composed of 4 bonding potentials (with the C-C bonds included), 2 spring potentials, an dihedral potential and torsional potentials - here, these individual components are not of great interest in the reaction coordinate of interest, yet require significant computational resources to analyse and recalculate with each timestep. By the CG procedure, multiple chains components can be approximate to a single bead, therefore speeding up the calculation of the equations of motion and convergence of physical properties. For example, Marrink et al estimated that the limit on biophysical MD simulations within reasonable timescales is 5000 AA lipids, or 50,000 CG lipids in CG models. A large range of AA and CG catalogues are avaliable, such as CHARMM/AMBER models for AA simulations, and MARTINI/SDK models to name a few.

The interaction of biomimetic membranes and nanoscale objects

Types of NPs and their environmental effects

Given the delicate environment of the cellular membrane, the question of how external nanoscale objects affect it's composition and geometry has been a question of great significance. Here, we follow the convention of simplifying these objects as nanoparticles (NPs). A large variety of NPs have been demonstrated to affect the membrane infrastructure. For example, carbon nanoparticles (CNPs) of nanometre (nm) scale diameter and radii can originate from diesel based soot in urban environments, and fine particulate matter in natural soot as well. A CNP of particular note is the fullerene structure, which consists of a hollow spherical allotrope. It's unique electronic and chemical properties has resulted in it's widescale use in industry, including it's potential as a source of nanotoxicity or a novel drug delivery mechanism - For example, clinical studies studying the effects of CNPs showed aggregation inside the lipid bilayer. Modifications to the CNP structure and geometry also has been demonstrated to affect it's cellular internalization mechanism. For example, carbon nanotubes (CNTs) are cyclindrical equivalents to the fullerene type NPs - the geometric anisotropy plays a critical role in it's material characteristics - CNTs of comparable size to a spherical CNP has been shown to undergo a bilayer translocation through a different mechanism, namely that of and larger ones go through a wrapping mechanism known as endocytosis, where the membrane curves to engulf the CNT, which required an input of energy. Another effect that is under investigation is the enhanced production of free radicals (molecular species that are highly reactive due to a unpaired electron) in the presence of CNPs - cellular components have natural defence mechanisms for combating excessive reactive oxidative species (ROS) (oxygen based free radical components) such as superoxide dimutase in the mitochondria. As CNPs have been demonstrated to aggregrate within the cellular environment, this provides a large surface area to mass ratio for the ROS to develop, which can result in an excess of ROS overcoming the natural defences of the cell. Environmental studies have also assessed that the cross-interaction between copper oxide (CuO) NPs and CNTs can hinder the toxic effects of the CuO NPs on microbial denitrification - an essential environmental process for fixing nitrogen into the environent. The evidence suggested that while CNT on its own does not affect the denitrification, the CuO-CNT interaction limits the NADH hindrance by the CuO NP, through the hindrance of the CuO uptake by the bacterial cellular membranes.

Surface Functionalization

Following the example set by the modification of rigid CNP/SiO NPs by functionalization, self-assembled monolayers (SAM) on a NP surface has been the basis of a promising field of materials research. Here, molecular simulation becomes a particularly powerful tool of analysis - the dependence of the integrity of a SAM-NP is highly sensitive on the pH, temperature, types of solvents and the lipid types involved in it's interaction. Molecular simulation provides an effective in-vivo comparison - the interaction between a NP surface ligand and every component inside the bilayer can be measured, which is still beyond modern microscopy and spectroscopic methods avaliable. Such modified NPs have been demonstrated as being powerful candidates for drug delivery. For example, AuNPs have been shown to aggregate in brain-tissue supported lipid bilayers (brain SLP) near regions where high densities of amyloid beta aggregates form, where the beta aggregates have been hypothesised as the primary cause of neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimers.
